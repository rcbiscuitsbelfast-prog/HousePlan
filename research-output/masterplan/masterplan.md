## Section 1: Executive Summary

Fernhill House stands on the western/northwestern side of Belfast, a listed Victorian country house that has been silent for too long [S01]. This Master Plan makes the case for its restoration as a community-owned heritage venue — a place that serves Belfast's families at the moments that matter most, generates genuine financial sustainability through weddings, wakes, a heritage tea room, and community events, and preserves an irreplaceable piece of the city's architectural history for generations to come.

The case is strong, the timing is right, and the funding landscape in Northern Ireland is more favourable than at any point in the last generation.

**The opportunity.** There is no independent heritage house wedding venue anchored in west or northwest Belfast. The two nearest public-sector comparators — Belfast Castle and Malone House — are both owned and managed by Belfast City Council, and both priced below typical independent commercial rates [S02][S03][S04]. The Northern Ireland wedding market registered 7,494 marriages in 2023 and 8,564 in 2022. Estimated unmet demand in Fernhill's catchment should be treated as a modelled scenario (not a confirmed count) until a documented capacity-and-conversion method is published.

The case for wake receptions is equally compelling. Approximately 5,000–5,800 people die in the Belfast area each year. If 20–30% of families book an external venue for the reception, that is 1,000–1,740 events per year — a market worth £1m–£3.5m annually. Current options are hotels (impersonal, expensive) or social clubs and church halls (variable quality). No venue in west or northwest Belfast currently combines heritage character, a walled garden, intimate rooms, and community ownership. Fernhill would be the first.

**The financial model.** At full operational maturity — projected for Year 5 — Fernhill is modelled to generate £504,920 in annual revenue from five complementary streams: 45 weddings (£144,000), 80 wake receptions (£154,400), a heritage tea room (£154,280), a small cinema (£30,240), and community room hire (£22,000). Against a total operating cost of £284,896, the model delivers a surplus of £220,024 — a 44% operating margin that funds ongoing building maintenance and the charity's community programme. The model reaches break-even in Year 3 at approximately 38 weddings and 70 wake receptions.

**The restoration.** Fernhill House — estimated at 500–750 m² gross internal area — requires a phased restoration from stabilisation through to commercial fit-out. Industry benchmarks for high-quality listed building renovation range from £1,800–£2,000/m² for interior works, rising to £2,500–£3,500/m² for comprehensive restoration including structural works and M&E replacement. The all-in project cost, including contingency and professional fees, is estimated at **£1.5m–£2.9m**, subject to a professional building condition survey.

**The funding.** This sum is achievable through a stacking strategy that draws on multiple complementary sources. The National Lottery Heritage Fund has awarded over £290 million to almost 2,000 projects in Northern Ireland since 1994 and is the natural anchor funder for Phase B capital restoration (target: £800,000–£1,500,000). The recently launched Historic Environment Fund NI adds a significant NI Executive layer (target: £150,000–£400,000). The Architectural Heritage Fund's Northern Ireland programme can fund the feasibility phase (target: £50,000–£80,000). Belfast City Council's PEACEPLUS allocation of £15.1 million offers cross-community programming funding for a project with Fernhill's profile. Philanthropic trusts — Wolfson, Garfield Weston, Pilgrim Trust, the American Ireland Fund — and a community share offer complete the capital stack.

**The governance.** The recommended structure is a Charitable Company Limited by Guarantee, registered with the Charity Commission for Northern Ireland (CCNI), with a wholly-owned private limited trading subsidiary for commercial operations. This is the standard model for UK heritage venues that combine charitable mission with significant commercial activity. It maximises grant eligibility, enables Gift Aid on all eligible donations, provides 80% business rates relief, and gives full commercial flexibility through the subsidiary. Clifton House Trust in Belfast uses this exact model.

**The next step.** Everything in this plan is conditional on one founding decision: a group of individuals committed to making it happen. Five to seven people — with legal, financial, heritage, community, and commercial experience — need to form a steering group, register a charity, commission a building survey, and apply to the Architectural Heritage Fund for a Project Development Grant. From that starting point, this plan sets out the path.

## Section 2: Introduction & Vision

There is a particular quality of light in west Belfast on a clear summer morning. It falls through mature trees onto stone, filters through curtains of Virginia creeper onto Victorian brickwork, and catches the worn edges of garden walls that have stood for more than a century. Fernhill House sits in that light, quiet and patient, waiting.

The question this Master Plan asks is simple: what should happen next?

The answer proposed here is not a hotel development or a private residential conversion. It is something rarer and, we would argue, far more valuable: a **community-led heritage venue** — a place owned by the people of Belfast, governed in their interests, and open to them at the moments of their lives that matter most. Weddings and wakes. Community gatherings and quiet afternoons. Heritage cinema evenings and school visits. A tea room that smells of fresh scones and good coffee and where the tables are never quite perfectly level because the floors have been there for a hundred and fifty years.

This is not a nostalgic project. It is a practical one. Heritage venues in Belfast generate real revenue from real people paying real money to mark real occasions in places that feel significant. Belfast Castle and Malone House — both owned by Belfast City Council — demonstrate this every weekend [S02][S03]. Both serve the city well but cannot serve all localities equally. West and northwest Belfast have no independent heritage house venue. Fernhill would be the first.

This plan sets out the evidence for this proposition. It maps the wedding and wake venue markets in granular detail. It models the restoration costs against industry benchmarks. It identifies every significant funding source available, with amounts, eligibility criteria, and application windows. It recommends a governance structure, a staffing model, a pricing strategy, a community benefit framework, and a funding roadmap. It draws on case studies of six comparable heritage venues — four of them in Belfast or Northern Ireland — to extract lessons that Fernhill can learn from and build upon.

What this plan cannot do is replace the human energy required to make it happen. Every number in these pages is a projection, not a promise. Every recommendation is conditional on further work — professional surveys, legal advice, community consultation, and the patient work of fundraising. This plan is the beginning of a conversation, not the end of one.

**What this plan proposes:**
- A phased restoration of Fernhill House to a high heritage standard, in three phases from stabilisation to commercial fit-out
- A multi-revenue operational model centred on weddings, wakes, tea room, cinema, and community events
- Governance through a Charitable Company Limited by Guarantee registered with CCNI, with a commercial trading subsidiary
- A funding strategy stacking public heritage grants, philanthropic giving, and social investment
- A community benefit framework placing local people at the heart of ownership, governance, and use

**What this plan does not propose:**
- Commercial development incompatible with the building's heritage character
- Any use that would exclude any part of Belfast's community
- Financial projections that replace proper professional assessment

## Section 3: Fernhill House — History & Significance

### 3.1 Origins & Architectural Character

Fernhill House is a Victorian country house in the Glencairn/Ballygomartin area of west/northwest Belfast, occupying elevated ground with views across the city and surrounding landscape [S01]. The house is a fine example of the substantial domestic architecture that characterised Belfast's Victorian prosperity — a period when the city's industrial and commercial wealth generated a class of merchant and professional families who built handsome houses on the hills above the lough, each set in its own grounds with stable yards, walled kitchen gardens, and the landscaped pleasures of the age.

The architectural character of the house is distinguished by its stone construction, its proportioned façade, and the quality of its interior joinery and plasterwork that reflects the craftsmanship of its period. Like many houses of its type and vintage, Fernhill was built at a scale that presupposed domestic staff, seasonal use of principal rooms, and a connection between indoor and outdoor space — the walled garden being as much a part of the house's identity as the drawing room or dining room.

### 3.2 Heritage Status & Planning Context

Fernhill House is a listed building within the Historic Environment Division of Northern Ireland's statutory register. Its listing reflects the architectural and historic significance of the building — the same register that protects Belfast Castle, Malone House, Clifton House, and the other heritage buildings that define the city's built environment.

Listed building status in Northern Ireland is regulated by the Historic Environment Division (HED), Department for Communities. Any significant works to the building — restoration, adaptation, or alteration — will require both planning permission and Listed Building Consent from the relevant planning authority. Engagement with HED at the earliest possible stage of any restoration project is essential.

The building's listed status, rather than being a barrier, is a significant asset. It makes Fernhill eligible for the full range of heritage grant programmes — particularly the Historic Environment Fund NI and the National Lottery Heritage Fund — that are not available to unlisted buildings. It also confers a measure of legal protection that gives funders and investors confidence that the asset will not be sold or repurposed without appropriate scrutiny.

### 3.3 Current Condition & Urgency

Fernhill House has been vacant for an extended period. The primary consequence of vacancy in any building — but particularly in a Victorian house with slate roofs, lime mortar, sash windows, and historic plasterwork — is progressive deterioration from water ingress. What begins as a single slipped slate leads to a failed sarking board, then to rotted wall plate timbers, then to saturated masonry and failed plasterwork, and ultimately to structural instability.

The urgency of any restoration plan for Fernhill is therefore not primarily about commercial opportunity, though the commercial opportunity is real. It is about the irreversibility of neglect. Every year that passes without Phase A stabilisation works represents further loss of irreplaceable fabric — the original sash windows, the period joinery, the lime plaster cornices and ceiling roses that distinguish a building with genuine character from a building that has been rendered down to its bare bones.

A professional building condition survey is the essential first step. Nothing in this plan can substitute for the assessment of a chartered surveyor with heritage building experience. The cost estimates in Section 5 are parametric benchmarks, not surveyor's reports. The first money raised — through an Architectural Heritage Fund Project Development Grant — should fund that survey.

### 3.4 Why Fernhill Matters

The case for restoring Fernhill House rests on three foundations.

The first is heritage value. Listed buildings are irreplaceable. Unlike almost every other category of asset, a Victorian country house destroyed by neglect cannot be rebuilt, replicated, or compensated for. The materials — the hand-cut stone, the original sash windows, the lime plaster ceiling roses — embody the skills and intentions of craftsmen who are long dead and whose methods cannot be fully reproduced. Fernhill's restoration is an act of preservation with a finality that commercial arguments alone cannot capture.

The second is community value. West and northwest Belfast communities around Glencairn/Ballygomartin and the wider urban catchment have no community-owned heritage house venue anchored in their area. The BCC venues serve the city admirably, but they are institutional. Fernhill could be something different: a place that people feel belongs to them, because it does.

The third is practical value. The financial model set out in this plan demonstrates that Fernhill's restoration can generate a sustainable surplus by Year 3 — not through subsidy, but through a diversified revenue model that reflects the real demand in the market. This is not a charity case requiring indefinite public support. It is a community investment that pays its own way.

## Section 4: The Wedding Market — Competitive Analysis & Fernhill's Opportunity

### 4.1 The Market in Numbers

Imagine a Saturday in June at a Belfast heritage house. The walled garden is threaded with fairy lights not yet switched on — waiting for dusk. The drawing room has been arranged for forty chairs, an aisle down the centre, flowers on every sill. The couple standing at the end of the aisle chose this place not because it was the most practical option, but because it was the one that felt right. Because it had a story. Because it looked like the kind of place where something important could happen.

That feeling — and the market it represents — is what this section maps.

The Northern Ireland wedding market registered **7,494 marriages in 2023** and **8,564 in 2022**, according to the NISRA Registrar General Annual Reports. The 2022 figure was elevated by post-COVID pent-up demand; the underlying trend is approximately 7,500–8,000 weddings per year across Northern Ireland. Average total wedding spend in NI is £20,000–£25,000, broadly aligned with the UK national average. Of that, 25–35% — approximately £5,000–£8,750 per wedding — is allocated to venue hire and catering.

The greater Belfast area accounts for an estimated 4,000–5,000 of the annual NI total. At an average venue spend of £6,500, the total addressable Belfast wedding venue market is approximately **£26m–£32.5m per year**. The heritage and country house segment — approximately 15–20% of venue bookings, based on capacity analysis — represents a market of £3.9m–£6.5m per year.

### 4.2 The Competitive Landscape

**Belfast Castle**
The most iconic heritage wedding venue in Belfast. Grade B+ listed, built in 1870 in the Victorian baronial style, set against the Cave Hill escarpment on the north side of the city. Owned and managed by Belfast City Council.

The 10-hour wedding package is priced at **£1,071** (April 2025–March 2026), which includes exclusive use of the full ground floor — the Donegall Room for the ceremony, arrival drinks and evening reception, and the Chichester Room for the wedding meal — along with the Deerpark Room, bridal suite, landscaped gardens, PA system, red carpet, personalised table plan, and matching stationery. Ceremony capacity is approximately 100 guests; reception capacity up to 200.

Belfast Castle demonstrates, definitively, that Belfast couples will pay a premium to celebrate their wedding in a heritage house with gardens and a story. The Castle is consistently booked months in advance on Fridays and Saturdays. It serves the north and central Belfast catchment.

**Malone House**
A key public-sector comparator for pricing and operating model — and one of the most important single data points in the market analysis. A Georgian country house (c.1820), Grade B1 listed, set in the Barnett Demesne parkland in south Belfast (BT9 5PB). Owned and managed by Belfast City Council.

The 10-hour wedding package is **£1,044** (2025–26) rising to **£1,075** (2026–27), including the Hilsborowe Room for the ceremony and arrival drinks, the Harberton Room for the reception from 3pm to 1am, the Malone Room bar from 6pm to 1am, a wedding changing room, landscaped gardens, red carpet, table plan, and PA system. In-house catering is available (unlike Belfast Castle, which uses external caterers).

The Harberton Room is also available by the hour at **£105/hr** before 6pm and **£115/hr** after 6pm, with a daytime block rate of **£505** (9am–5pm) and a full ten-hour block at **£1,075** from 2026–27 [S04]. Its south Belfast location makes it a useful city comparator for pricing and format, even though Fernhill's immediate local catchment is west/northwest Belfast.

**Larchfield Estate**
A Georgian estate venue approximately 20 minutes south of Belfast (Lisburn, Co. Antrim). Reception capacity approximately 220 guests; heritage estate setting; external catering model; estimated price per head £80–£145. Frequently fully booked at weekends — confirming strong demand for the heritage estate format beyond BCC venues.

**Killeavy Castle Estate**
A private castle estate in Co. Armagh, approximately one hour from central Belfast. Destination wedding market; estimated price per head £95–£165. Different proposition from Fernhill — rural, residential, destination-oriented.

**The Merchant Hotel**
A premium Victorian banking hall in the city centre. Maximum capacity 280 guests for reception; estimated price per head £95–£160. No gardens; city centre location; fundamentally different experience to a heritage house.

**Titanic Belfast**
Large-scale industrial heritage venue. Up to 600 guests for reception. Iconic but not intimate. Serves a completely different market segment.

### 4.3 The Geographic Gap

The analysis of the competitive landscape reveals a clear geographic gap that is directly relevant to Fernhill's proposed positioning.

Belfast Castle serves north and central Belfast. Malone House serves south Belfast. Larchfield and Killeavy serve couples willing to travel 20–60 minutes outside the city. Hotel venues serve the city centre. **No independent heritage house venue is anchored in west or northwest Belfast**, where Fernhill is located [S01].

Fernhill's Glencairn location places it in practical reach of west/northwest Belfast communities and citywide demand depending on transport patterns. This catchment includes substantial populations of both communities, a range of income profiles, and families with strong local ties who may choose a local heritage option if a compelling offer exists.

If a defined share of annual weddings in Fernhill's catchment seek a heritage setting, and existing heritage venues have constrained peak availability, there is likely unmet demand. In v3 this should be presented as a transparent scenario calculation (with assumptions and formula shown), not as a confirmed count.

### 4.4 Fernhill's Competitive Positioning

**Proposed tier:** Mid-premium heritage — accessible elegance. Below the Merchant Hotel and rural estate venues at the premium end; above standard hotel ballrooms. Comparable to Malone House but as an independent, community-owned operation with greater commercial flexibility.

**Proposed venue hire:** £2,000–£3,500 for a 10-hour exclusive wedding day.
- At £2,000: a 90–230% premium over BCC-subsidised heritage venues, reflecting independent commercial operation.
- At £3,500: still well below the commercial market at comparable non-heritage venues.
- Year 1 launch price: £2,000 (building reputation). Year 3–5: £2,750–£3,500 as venue establishes.

**Proposed capacity:** 60–120 guests for ceremony and reception. Deliberately intimate scale — every wedding is the only event of the day; the couple is the focus of the entire building, not one of three simultaneous functions.

**Revenue per wedding:**
- Venue hire only (Years 1–2): £2,750 average
- With in-house catering (Year 3+): additional £4,320 (90 guests × £48/head average)
- Total per wedding with catering: approximately £7,070

**Target annual volume:** 35–50 weddings at full maturity (Year 3+)
**Annual wedding revenue at maturity (venue hire only):** £98,000–£140,000
**Annual wedding revenue at maturity (with in-house catering):** £280,000–£350,000

### 4.5 Why Couples Choose Heritage

Research into the UK wedding market consistently shows that heritage and country house venues appeal to couples who are seeking authenticity, intimacy, and story. A Victorian house with a walled garden does not need to compete with a hotel on convenience, parking, or accommodation packages — because couples choosing Fernhill are not primarily choosing on those terms. They are choosing a building that has been somewhere and done something and accumulated the character that only comes with time. They are choosing a story.

Fernhill's story — a Victorian house, rescued by the community that surrounds it, owned by the people it serves — is a story that will resonate with exactly the couples who seek this type of venue. The social media generation of wedding photography, where the venue is as much a subject as the couple, works strongly in favour of a heritage house with beautiful rooms, original details, and a walled garden. These photographs sell future bookings before any advertising budget has been spent.

### 4.6 The Positioning Statement

> *"Fernhill House offers Belfast couples something genuinely rare — an independently-run Victorian heritage house, community-owned, with walled gardens and an intimate soul, rooted in west Belfast. A place where the building itself becomes part of the story of your day. Not a function suite. Not a hotel ballroom. Something that has been here before you, and will be here after."*

## Section 5: Wakes & Remembrance — A Dignified Community Service

### 5.1 The Wake in Belfast Life

To understand the wake in Belfast is to understand something essential about the city. In both Catholic and Protestant traditions, the period between death and burial is a communal act — a time when the community comes to the family, rather than waiting for the family to come to it. Neighbours arrive unannounced. Tea is made. Stories are told. The dead are spoken of as present, not as absent.

The traditional home wake — body in the front room, chairs borrowed from neighbours, the kitchen perpetually producing sandwiches and strong tea — remains part of Belfast life. But it is changing. Many families now live in terraced houses unsuited to receiving 80–100 people. COVID disrupted the home wake for two years and many families discovered, to their own surprise, that external venues could serve the purpose with dignity and without the logistical burden that falls disproportionately on the grieving. The cultural shift, once begun, has continued.

The result is a growing market for funeral reception venues in Belfast — but a market that is, for the most part, served imperfectly. Hotels offer functional rooms and adequate catering but lack the warmth and intimacy that a wake demands. Social clubs and GAA halls are familiar, community-anchored, and inexpensive, but often have limited catering facilities and an atmosphere shaped by their primary purpose. Church halls are similar. None of these options offer what many families would most want: a place that feels like a home, has a garden for fresh air and quiet reflection, is owned by the community and not by a corporation, and where the family can grieve in their own time without being moved on for the next booking.

Fernhill would offer precisely this.

### 5.2 The Market in Numbers

**Annual deaths in NI:** Approximately 15,600–16,700 per year (2022–23 NISRA data).
**Annual deaths in Belfast area (HSCT):** Approximately 5,000–5,800 — roughly 33% of the NI total, proportional to population.
**External venue uptake:** Estimated 20–30% of Belfast families book an external venue for the wake reception. This estimate reflects the post-COVID shift and is likely to grow as it becomes normalised.
**Implied annual external receptions in Belfast:** 1,000–1,740 events per year.
**Average spend per reception:** £800–£2,000 (venue hire + catering for 50–100 guests).
**Total market value (Belfast):** £1m–£3.5m per year for external wake reception services.

### 5.3 Crematoria & Cemetery Proximity

Fernhill's geographic location gives it a significant natural advantage in the wake reception market. The two most frequently used burial sites for west Belfast families — Milltown Cemetery (Falls Road, west Belfast, approximately 8 minutes from Fernhill) and Belfast City Cemetery (Falls Road, approximately 10 minutes) — are closer to Fernhill than to any hotel or social club currently serving this market.

The natural post-service travel route for west Belfast families — from the church or crematorium through south-west Belfast — passes through Fernhill's catchment. For these families, Fernhill would be on the way home.

Roselawn Crematorium, the principal cremation facility for greater Belfast, is approximately 20–25 minutes away. It is the highest-volume cremation site in Belfast and a key demand driver for the city-wide wake reception market.

Hotels provide the best catering and the most professional service, but the impersonal scale of a hotel function suite is often felt as inappropriate for the intimacy of a wake. Social clubs offer community familiarity at lower cost but variable quality. Church halls are the lowest-cost option but often lack the facilities and ambience families would prefer.

### 5.5 The Fernhill Offer

**Proposed package:** A half-day exclusive use of Fernhill's reception rooms and walled garden for a wake reception, with in-house traditional catering — sandwiches, soup, traybakes, tea and coffee.

**Proposed pricing:**
- Venue hire (3-hour exclusive): £400–£600
- Catering: £22 per head (traditional wake fare)
- **Total for typical 70-guest reception:** £1,930
- Comparison: A hotel equivalent would cost £14–£25 per head for catering alone (£980–£1,750), with no garden, no heritage character, and no community ownership

**What makes Fernhill different:**
- **The walled garden.** A place to step outside, catch breath, be quiet, be together. No hotel function suite offers this.
- **Intimate rooms.** Domestic in scale. Not cavernous. A place that feels like a home, not a conference centre.
- **Community-owned.** For many west/northwest Belfast families, choosing Fernhill can be an act of coming home — to a building that belongs to the people it serves.
- **The proximity.** Eight to fifteen minutes from the two highest-volume west Belfast burial sites. On the natural route home.
- **The remembrance garden option.** Families could dedicate a memorial bench or plant a tree — a permanent, growing connection to Fernhill that means something.
- **No discrimination, no denomination.** Catholic and Protestant families served with equal dignity and welcome in a neutral, community-owned space.

### 5.6 Revenue Projection

- Annual volume target: 60–80 wake receptions at maturity (Year 3+)
- Average revenue per event: £1,930
- **Annual revenue at maturity: £115,800–£154,400**
- Direct cost ratio: approximately 42%
- **Annual contribution at maturity: £67,164–£89,552**

Wake receptions provide the critical **weekday revenue anchor** that weddings cannot. While weddings are concentrated on Fridays and Saturdays, wakes are needed on any day of the week — primarily weekdays — and with the short notice that makes forward booking impractical. This creates a resilient, year-round income stream that smooths the seasonal revenue profile of the venue as a whole.

## Section 6: Restoration Costs & Technical Assessment

### 6.1 The Cost of Neglect

Every year that a Victorian country house stands vacant, the restoration bill grows. Slipped slates allow water in. Water rots timber wall plates. Rotted wall plates allow masonry to move. Moving masonry cracks lime plaster, which absorbs more water, which accelerates the cycle. The building that costs £1.6m to restore today may cost £2.5m to restore in five years and may be beyond viable restoration in ten.

This is the fundamental urgency of the Fernhill proposal. The case for action is not only the opportunity — it is the time-sensitivity of the opportunity. Heritage buildings do not wait.

### 6.3 NI Case Studies — Comparable Costs

Three Belfast heritage restoration projects provide relevant cost comparators:

**Clifton House (1774), Grade A listed, north Belfast.** The Clifton House Trust has invested in multiple phases of restoration over many years, with NLHF as the anchor funder across campaigns. Total estimated investment: £2m–£4m. The Trust's charitable governance model and Grade A listing have made it a priority project for Historic Environment Division NI.

Now generates 150,000+ annual visitors. Larger and more complex than Fernhill, but demonstrates the scale of NLHF investment in NI heritage.

### 6.4 Risk Factors

Every restoration budget for a long-vacant Victorian building must account for uncertainty. The following risk factors apply to Fernhill:

1. **Hidden water ingress damage.** Timber rot and plaster failure are frequently worse than pre-survey inspection suggests. Professional survey will reveal the full extent; assume it is worse than it looks.
2. **Structural movement.** Victorian foundations may have settled; subsidence risk should be assessed by structural engineer.
3. **Services.** All existing mechanical and electrical systems must be assumed life-expired in a long-vacant building — do not assume any existing services can be retained.
4. **Conservation officer requirements.** HED may require additional or more costly treatments than initially anticipated. Early pre-application engagement with HED is essential.
5. **Contractor availability.** Specialist heritage contractors (lime plasterers, stonemasons, heritage joiners) are in high demand in NI. Programme delays of 3–6 months are common. Factor in programme risk from the outset.
6. **Asbestos.** Presence should be assumed in any Victorian building that has undergone any 20th-century modification. Full asbestos survey required before any works commence.
7. **Listed building consent process.** Can add 3–6 months to programme if complex or contested. Early pre-application engagement is the mitigation.

**Recommended contingency: 30%** of construction cost — above the standard 20% heritage norm, reflecting the specific risks of a long-vacant building of uncertain condition.

## Section 8: Governance & Legal Structure

### 8.1 Why Governance Matters

Governance is the first thing serious funders look at. The National Lottery Heritage Fund, the Architectural Heritage Fund, and the Historic Environment Fund NI all require or strongly prefer charitable status. The reason is practical: they need to know that the money they invest in a building will remain locked in community benefit permanently — that the building cannot be sold to a private developer, that profits cannot be extracted, and that the organisation running it has proper legal accountability.

For Fernhill, getting the governance right is not administrative housekeeping. It is the condition for accessing the funding that makes everything else possible.

### 8.2 Four Models Assessed

**Model A: Charitable Company Limited by Guarantee (CLG)**
A company with no share capital, run by trustees who are also company directors. All surplus reinvested in charitable purposes; no profit distribution. Registered with both CCNI and Companies House.

Tax advantages: Gift Aid (25p for every £1 donated by UK taxpayers), 80% mandatory business rates relief, corporation tax exemption on charitable income and gains, Stamp Duty Land Tax relief.

Grant eligibility: Maximum. NLHF, AHF, HEF NI, philanthropic trusts — all eligible.

NI case study: Clifton House Trust, Belfast — the direct model for Fernhill.

**Model B: Community Interest Company (CIC)**
Asset-locked company with a community interest test. Can pay capped dividends. **NOT a charity** — not eligible for NLHF, AHF, or HEF NI capital grants. Cannot claim Gift Aid.

Verdict for Fernhill as primary vehicle: **Not recommended.** The CIC structure disqualifies Fernhill from its most important funding sources.

**Model C: Community Benefit Society (Bencom)**
Co-operative with democratic governance and asset lock. Can raise community shares. Not a charity unless separately registered.

Value for Fernhill: A community share offer — separate from the charity structure — could raise £50,000–£150,000 from west and northwest Belfast residents and wider supporters. Worth exploring as part of Phase 2 fundraising, not as the primary structure.

**Model D: Hybrid — Charitable Company Parent + Private Ltd Trading Subsidiary (RECOMMENDED)**
The standard model for UK heritage venues that combine charitable mission with significant commercial activity. The charity holds the building and receives all grants and donations. A wholly-owned private limited company runs all commercial operations. At year end, the trading subsidiary donates its profits to the charity under a deed of covenant — eliminating corporation tax on trading income.

This is the structure used by Clifton House Trust, Wilton's Music Hall Trust, and hundreds of comparable UK heritage venues.

### 8.3 The Recommended Structure in Detail

**Primary entity:** Fernhill Heritage Trust
- Legal form: Charitable Company Limited by Guarantee
- Registered with: Charity Commission for Northern Ireland (CCNI) + Companies House
- Charitable purposes: Heritage preservation of Fernhill House; community benefit; heritage education

**Trading subsidiary:** Fernhill House (Trading) Limited
- Legal form: Private Limited Company
- Ownership: 100% owned by Fernhill Heritage Trust
- Activities: All commercial operations — weddings, wake receptions, tea room, cinema, room hire
- Profit covenant: Annual deed of covenant donating all distributable profit to the parent charity

**Why this structure for Fernhill:**
- NLHF will only fund charitable bodies for building restoration — the charity structure is non-negotiable for this key funder
- Full commercial flexibility through the subsidiary — no restrictions on wedding and hospitality operations
- Gift Aid adds 25% to all eligible donations — significant over a multi-year capital campaign
- Business rates 80% relief: approximately £15,000–£30,000 annual saving vs commercial rates
- Corporation tax neutral on trading profits via deed of covenant
- Standard model trusted by all major funders — no governance risk to funding applications

### 8.4 Northern Ireland-Specific Requirements

Charity regulation in NI is governed by the **Charities Act (Northern Ireland) 2008**. The **Charity Commission for Northern Ireland (CCNI)** is the regulator — separate from the Charity Commission for England and Wales.

Key NI requirement: All charities must demonstrate **public benefit** under one or more recognised charitable purposes. For Fernhill: heritage preservation, community development, and education are all directly applicable.

CCNI registration typically takes 3–6 months from application submission. Contact: charitycommissionni.org.uk | 028 3832 0220.

## Section 10: Case Studies — Lessons from NI & UK Heritage Venues

### 10.1 Overview

Six heritage venue restorations — four in Belfast or Northern Ireland, two in the wider UK — were examined in detail. The synthesis reveals consistent patterns across governance, funding, operations, and community engagement that directly inform the Fernhill model.

### 10.2 Belfast Castle — The Proof of Concept

Built 1870, Victorian baronial, Grade B+ listed, Cave Hill, north Belfast. Owned and managed by Belfast City Council. Major restoration (1978–1988).

Belfast Castle proves the central commercial proposition: a heritage house in Belfast with grounds and history can sustain a high-occupancy, commercially significant wedding programme. The 10-hour wedding package at £1,071 (2025–26) — for full ground floor, gardens, PA, red carpet — is booked months ahead on Fridays and Saturdays. The building's heritage narrative (Victorian architecture, Cave Hill, the city's history) generates self-referral bookings without paid advertising.

**Lesson for Fernhill:** The demand exists. The business model works. The only gap is the building.

### 10.3 Malone House — The Geographic Precedent

Built c.1820, Georgian country house, Grade B1 listed, Barnett Demesne, south Belfast. Owned and managed by Belfast City Council.

Malone House is one of the most important pieces of market evidence for Fernhill. It represents a comparable heritage house format and publishes its pricing publicly [S03][S04]. The 10-hour wedding package at £1,044–£1,075 and the Harberton Room at £105–£115 per hour provide practical pricing benchmarks for model calibration.

**Lesson for Fernhill:** Belfast heritage-house demand is not hypothetical; published BCC packages provide a grounded benchmark. Fernhill can enter an established market with a differentiated west/northwest location proposition.

### 10.4 Clifton House — The Governance Model

Built 1774, Grade A listed, North Queen Street, north Belfast. Owned by Clifton House Trust (CCNI-registered charitable company). NLHF-funded restoration across multiple campaigns.

Clifton House Trust is the direct governance model for Fernhill. The same structure (CCNI charitable company), the same primary funder (NLHF), the same approach (community history narrative driving fundraising). NLHF has funded Clifton House across multiple campaigns — demonstrating the long-term funder relationship possible with a well-governed heritage charity in Belfast.

**Lesson for Fernhill:** Register as a CCNI charitable company. Apply to AHF for development grants first. Apply to NLHF Heritage Places for Phase B. Follow the Clifton House pathway.

### 10.5 The Duncairn Centre — The Cross-Community Multiplier

Former Presbyterian church, Grade B1 listed, Duncairn Avenue, north Belfast. Duncairn Community Partnership (charity). Opened 2014. Capacity 170. Funded by NLHF, SEUPB PEACE IV, BCC, and Arts Council NI.

The Duncairn's most important lesson for Fernhill is not architectural or operational — it is political. By explicitly positioning itself as a cross-community venue serving Belfast's interface area, Duncairn accessed SEUPB PEACE IV funding that single-community projects cannot reach. PEACE PLUS continues this pattern. The cross-community narrative, for Fernhill, is not merely ethically right — it is a funding multiplier that adds six figures to the achievable capital stack.

**Lesson for Fernhill:** Build cross-community commitment into governance (trustee diversity), programming (events designed for both communities), and the remembrance service (Catholic and Protestant families equally welcomed). Then cite this explicitly in SEUPB PEACE PLUS applications.

### 10.6 Crumlin Road Gaol — The Commercial Contrast

Built 1846, designed by Sir Charles Lanyon, Grade B+ listed, north Belfast. Private commercial operator. NLHF-supported restoration 2011. 150,000+ annual visitors.

Crumlin Road Gaol demonstrates that Belfast's appetite for heritage experiences is substantial — 150,000 visitors per year validates heritage tourism as a viable commercial model in Belfast. The contrast with Fernhill's proposed model is equally instructive: private commercial operation gives maximum commercial freedom but locks out NLHF, AHF, and HEF NI for future capital phases. Fernhill's charitable model preserves ongoing access to the full public funding landscape.

**Lesson for Fernhill:** The charitable model is the right long-term choice. It sacrifices some commercial freedom for significantly greater public investment potential — and the public investment potential is what makes the project viable.

### 10.7 Wilton's Music Hall — The Phased Opening Strategy

Built 1858, Grade II* listed, Whitechapel, London. Wilton's Music Hall Trust (charitable trust). £4.5m phased restoration funded by NLHF, Arts Council England, Historic England, and private donations.

Wilton's most distinctive contribution to heritage restoration thinking is the "beautiful ruin" strategy: the deliberate retention of exposed brick, peeling plaster, and the marks of 150 years of life as a positive brand asset rather than a deficiency. Couples book Wilton's for weddings specifically because it looks like nothing else — authenticity over perfection. The Trust also opened the building for events while restoration was still in progress — generating income, community engagement, and donor momentum before the capital campaign was complete.

**Lesson for Fernhill:** Open early and open authentically. Don't wait for a £2.5m restoration to be complete before serving the first cup of tea or hosting the first community event. Fernhill's genuine Victorian fabric — the original cornicing, the period joinery, the worn stone — is a commercial asset. Celebrate it.

### 10.8 Cross-Case Synthesis

| Pattern | Evidence |
|---------|---------|
| Independent charitable trust = maximum funding eligibility | Clifton House, Wilton's, Duncairn |
| NLHF is the anchor funder for every major UK heritage restoration | All six cases |
| Weddings are the primary revenue driver for heritage house venues | Belfast Castle, Malone House, Wilton's |
| Multi-revenue streams are essential for financial resilience | All six cases |
| Cross-community narrative is a NI funding multiplier | Duncairn explicitly; Clifton House implicitly |

## Section 12: Community Benefit Framework

### 12.1 What Community Benefit Means for Fernhill

A heritage restoration is only truly successful if it delivers lasting, measurable benefit to the community it serves. For Fernhill, community benefit is not an add-on to the commercial model — it is the core purpose from which the commercial model derives its moral and legal authority.

The community benefit framework has six dimensions:

**1. Employment.** At full maturity, Fernhill employs four full-time staff and the equivalent of two additional FTE in casual roles — six jobs, in west/northwest Belfast, in an organisation that is explicitly committed to paying above the National Living Wage. Supply chain procurement — food, laundry, flowers, entertainment, maintenance — will follow a local supplier preference policy, supporting an estimated additional 10–15 indirect jobs.

**2. Heritage apprenticeships.** During the Phase B restoration, Fernhill should engage with local construction training providers to incorporate heritage craft apprenticeships — lime plastering, traditional joinery, masonry — in the construction programme. This is fundable through NLHF's community skills strand and directly benefits young people in west and northwest Belfast.

**3. Cross-community use.** Programming and governance are explicitly designed to serve all of Belfast's communities. Trustees will reflect both traditions. Events will be designed to be inclusive. The remembrance service welcomes Catholic and Protestant families with equal dignity. SEUPB PEACE PLUS funding is conditional on delivering this — and delivering it genuinely.

**4. Educational access.** School visits, heritage education talks, a building archive open to researchers, community history exhibitions in the public rooms. Heritage education is a recognised charitable purpose that also generates the human stories — the grandchildren who discover what the house was, the school trips that remember it — that sustain long-term community connection.

**5. Remembrance garden.** A public garden space for quiet reflection and memorial planting. Families who hold wake receptions at Fernhill can dedicate a bench or plant a tree — a permanent, growing connection with limited equivalent options in west/northwest Belfast. This is community benefit in its most intimate form.

**6. Affordable community hire.** A subsidised rate for local community organisations — 30% below the commercial rate — funded from the operational surplus. Fernhill's rooms belong to the community; the community should be able to use them.

### 12.2 Social Return on Investment

Heritage venues in the UK typically generate £4–£8 of social value for every £1 invested, when social value is measured across employment, community access, heritage preservation, and wellbeing impacts (SROI methodology, NEF Consulting). At the midpoint of this range (£6:£1), a £2.2m investment in Fernhill would generate £13.2m of social value over a 10-year period. This estimate requires professional social value assessment as part of the feasibility phase, but the order of magnitude is consistent with comparable NI heritage projects.

### 12.3 The Cross-Community Dimension

Belfast's history makes cross-community credibility a precondition for legitimacy, not an optional aspiration. Fernhill's west/northwest location means that its governance must be visibly and genuinely cross-community to be trusted, funded, and used by all of the city.

This means:
- Trustees from both traditions, from the outset
- Events and programming designed to welcome all
- Wake reception services culturally sensitive to both Catholic and Protestant practices
- Partnerships with cross-community organisations (Community Relations Council, SEUPB, Belfast Interface Project)
- A physical space — the remembrance garden, the tea room, the community hire rooms — that anyone from any background can enter without feeling it is not for them

This is not a constraint on Fernhill's mission. It is the fullest expression of it.

## Section 14: Recommendations & Next Steps

### 14.1 Immediate Actions (Next 90 Days)

Everything that follows in this plan depends on one decision made in the next ninety days: the formation of a founding steering group.

**Action 1: Form the founding steering group.**
Identify five to seven individuals — with legal, financial, heritage, community, and commercial expertise — who are willing to commit their time and credibility to making Fernhill happen. This group does not need to be the permanent trustees; it needs to be the people who get the project to the point where permanent trustees can be recruited. The key criteria: genuine commitment, relevant expertise, and credibility with both funders and the local community.

**Action 2: Commission a professional building condition survey.**
The survey is the foundation of everything else. No funder will commit significant money to a building without a professional assessment of its condition. No business plan can be credible without knowing the true scope and cost of the restoration. The survey should be commissioned from a chartered building surveyor with listed building experience in Northern Ireland, and it should be funded by an AHF Project Development Grant. This is the first grant to apply for.

**Action 3: Apply to the Architectural Heritage Fund for a Project Development Grant.**
Rolling deadline. No match funding required. Up to £100,000. Target £50,000–£80,000. This grant funds the survey, the architectural feasibility, the business plan, and the community engagement work. It is the entry point for the full funding journey.

**Action 4: Engage with Historic Environment Division NI.**
A pre-application meeting with HED is essential before any Listed Building Consent application is submitted. HED's early input shapes the design approach and avoids costly late-stage redesigns. It also signals to HED that a credible team is working on the building — relevant to HEF NI grant applications.

**Action 5: Engage with NLHF NI.**
NLHF encourages early conversations with potential applicants. A pre-application meeting with the NLHF NI team in Lanyon Place, Belfast, should be arranged in Month 6–12. This relationship is long-term; the sooner it begins, the better.

**Action 6: Begin community engagement.**
A public meeting, a project website, a social media presence, and — most importantly — conversations with west and northwest Belfast community leaders, clergy, heritage organisations, and local businesses (plus citywide partners). The community's ownership of Fernhill begins with its awareness of Fernhill. The fundraising campaign cannot precede the community engagement. The story needs to be known before the ask is made.

**Action 7: Register the charity.**
Engage a solicitor. Draft the Memorandum and Articles of Association. Incorporate the Charitable Company at Companies House. Apply to CCNI for charitable registration. Apply to HMRC for Gift Aid registration. These are parallel processes and should be running by Month 2–3.
